### What the Agency Run Did Better: Narrative Innovation

The Agency board's judges produced three design innovations that no other run discovered:

1. **The Tomas interruption.** A mid-session event where a kid gets dragged underwater regardless of which path the party is on. They must choose: dive in (costs 30+ min of clock) or stay on task (Tomas's mother screams his name for the rest of the session, townspeople refuse further help). This is sophisticated adventure design — a forced tradeoff between the main quest and moral obligation that has no right answer.

2. **Faction fallout.** Whichever thread resolves the rescue, the surviving faction retaliates within the hour. This creates a 4th act that extends the consequences of the party's choices beyond the climax. Most one-shots end when the boss dies — this one doesn't.

3. **"Elda doesn't want to leave."** The rescue target refuses rescue because the villain's ritual is keeping her alive. The party must choose: honor her wish (she lives but Maren gets no closure), drag her out (she dies within days), or negotiate. This subverts the entire rescue premise and creates genuine moral complexity.

These are ideas that emerge from creative intuition — the judges imagining "what would make this session unforgettable?" They're not derived from design best practices; they're original design choices.

### What the Investigation Run Did Better: Practical Runnability

The investigation judges researched DND adventure design best practices (cited: The Angry GM, World Anvil, Mythcreants, Flutes Loot) and applied them systematically:

1. **Keyed dungeon rooms.** 4 rooms with DCs, ACs, hazards, cover rules, and tactical notes. The main combat location went from one paragraph to a usable tactical space. A DM knows exactly what's in each room.

2. **Boxed read-aloud text.** 4 atmospheric paragraphs (dock, wreck, caves, temple) that the DM reads aloud. These didn't exist in any previous run. They're a standard module feature that the investigation judges identified as "highest impact for runnability."

3. **Convergence outcome table.** An explicit grid showing what happens based on which choices the party made. The Agency run described this in prose — the investigation run made it a scannable table. Much more DM-friendly during play.

4. **Cross-path bridges section.** Explicit mechanics for how NPCs connect paths: Mirrek provides wreck intel and joins the assault, Asha saves 4 hours of travel, Vael provides Water Breathing but plants a spy. The Agency run implied path connections — the investigation run specified them.

5. **Complete mechanical scaffolding.** Underwater rules, skill challenge consequences, loot table, stat blocks for minor NPCs, ritual clock with rest costs. A DM opens this document and has everything needed to run every encounter.

### Why They Differ

The judges optimized for different things because they investigated different questions:

**Agency judges asked:** "What would make this adventure memorable at the table?" They imagined play experiences and designed for emotional impact — the gut-punch of Tomas drowning, the twist of Elda refusing rescue, the fallout of surviving factions. These are creative insights from imagining the table experience.

**Investigation judges asked:** "What does a well-designed one-shot need?" They read adventure design guides and identified structural gaps — no keyed rooms, no read-aloud, no mechanical consequences, no outcome table. These are craft insights from researching best practices.

Both approaches produce good adventures. The Agency adventure is more *surprising*. The Investigation adventure is more *complete*.

## Summary: Investigation Judges' Signature

Across both creative tasks, the investigation judges produce artifacts with a distinctive character:

1. **Practical over surprising.** The DND adventure is more runnable but less innovative. The fantasy prose has more structural layers but less focused polish. Investigation judges optimize for completeness and structural soundness.

2. **Research-informed.** The DND judges cited adventure design guides. The fantasy judges did sentence-by-sentence close reading and comparative calibration against published authors. The ASI proposals reference evidence, not intuition.

3. **Faster convergence.** The DND adventure went from outline (5.3 specificity) to runnable module (8.3) in 3 iterations. The fantasy prose found its second tension axis by iteration 2. Investigation frontloads understanding, which reduces wasted iterations.

4. **Structural improvement over creative innovation.** The investigation judges add keyed rooms, outcome tables, read-aloud text, field-report tics, cross-path mechanics. The Agency judges add mid-session interruptions, NPC twists, faction dynamics. Both are valuable — they optimize for different things.

## Recommendation

The ideal simmer configuration depends on the task:

| Task Type | Best Configuration | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Technical (evaluator-backed) | Investigation judges (builtin) | Discovers scoring mechanics and GT structure immediately; fastest convergence |
| Creative (narrative innovation priority) | Agency board | Produces surprising design choices from creative intuition |
| Creative (craft/completeness priority) | Investigation judges | Research-informed structural improvements; more polished output |
| Production module (must be runnable) | Investigation judges | Fills every mechanical gap; DM runs cold |
| Portfolio piece (must be memorable) | Agency board | Creates moments that stick (Tomas, "Elda doesn't want to leave", "unutilized") |

Both work well. The investigation flow is the bigger structural improvement to simmer because it applies universally — every task benefits from judges understanding the problem before proposing fixes. Agency composition adds creative sparkle on top of that foundation.
